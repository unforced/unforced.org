This is a process companion to [*Two Years at ATLAS*](/writing/two-years-at-atlas/). I'm using AI more and more as a thinking and writing partner — for this piece, that meant working with Claude through several drafts. I want to be transparent about that, so I'm starting to share process notes for pieces where AI was substantially involved. The aim isn't to claim or disclaim — it's to show.

What follows is the journey. The free-write came first, written stream-of-consciousness without much structure. Claude responded with what it heard as the strongest signal, plus a structural outline. I wrote a first draft using that as scaffolding, then we iterated together to the version that's [now published](/writing/two-years-at-atlas/).

## The free-write

*Verbatim, unedited. This was the starting point — me working through the territory without trying to shape it yet.*

This weekend I graduate from CU Boulder with a masters in Creative Technology & Design from the ATLAS Institute. I began this program almost two years ago with an intention of giving myself a container to really explore myself as a technology builder again, but also to deepen my connection with CU. I decided to return to school shortly after I made a run for Boulder city council at the end of 2023. Campaigning for city council helped ignite a fire in me around what Boulder can be, which led me to want to get more connected with CU Boulder, as it is one of the key places in Boulder. I had already been connected with Naropa University, having gotten my first masters degree there studying Ecopsychology. I always love exploring where things meet and getting an education from both Naropa and from CU gave me two different lenses into education systems but also two different lenses into the wider community in Boulder and the various participants in it.

Further, my masters thesis from Naropa had been "Radical Reorganization", exploring a shift in paradigms for our society and how this happens throughout our various social systems. Within this, as I do enjoy looking at the whole, I also always want to be understanding my particular place in it. And it's become increasingly clear that one of the unique gifts I hold is the ability to hold this living system embodied and relational perspective, while also being deeply enmeshed in technology and understanding deeply how it works and being very capable of building with it. I often have people reflect back how surprised they are when they learn that I am in tech because I am such a free spirited and loving individual. But regardless, it's just clear to me that contributing to the future of technology is a role I am uniquely situated to contribute towards, and it's something I care a lot about, and find deep fulfillment in. And so in part going to CU was my way of putting myself back in that context more fully, so I could have a frame for two years that would support me growing in dimensions that I hadn't been tending to as much. I think this was one of the core things I came for, just to have a frame in which to operate for a bit, and to be able to connect into the network that was framed by that, the various students and professors and others who are part of CU and especially part of ATLAS. I actually originally applied to do a PhD at ATLAS, seeking to do a combined PhD in Creative Technology & Design as well as Cognitive Science. I think this was really driven by a desire to start to do something bigger. I've got big ambitions about where our society can go, and this intersection of Consciousness and Technology has long been a huge interest for me. It's actually one of the root intersections that is at the heart of the non-profit I've been running, Woven Web, which was called Consciousness Hacking Colorado when I stepped in as Executive Director. And so finding more ways to explore and participate and contribute at this intersection has felt deeply meaningful, and as I always aspire towards doing big things, I figured working towards a PhD at this intersection would be very meaningful and a lot of fun. As I considered what I might focus my studies on, I found myself gearing more and more towards the concept of learning. I've always been very interested in how we learn, and I see myself as a life long learner. I aspire to learn more every day and I don't really see any area of life as off limits to me, even areas like music where I haven't found myself to be as naturally talented or as dedicated to a practice, I think that I will one day be able to be great. I think a lot of this is driven from a deep belief in the innate potential of human beings, and the idea that when we understand ourselves beyond all the specificities, that we can learn anything much more effectively. I suppose I may have taken the matrix movie a bit too seriously when Neo plugged himself in and just downloaded kung-fu programs. But I also think that I can see the rate of growth of technology more clearly than most people and it leads me to think that something like that is becoming more and more possible. And it excites me about what it might mean for my individual experience as a human but also for our path as humanity on this earth. And it also motivates me to want to contribute in a meaningful way towards understanding how we learn and how we can do that more effectively. And as a technologist, I of course often think about what technology can contribute to that. I didn't ultimately get accepted into the PhD program but I did get accepted into the MS. And it turns out that two years in a technology container was an ideal way for me to grow exactly what I wanted to grow, and indirectly I got to start explroing more of my ideas around learning.

Over the course of this program, I played around a lot with new ways of using AI for technology development, which has - to me - been one of the most exciting new directions of technology in our generation, and this masters program has given me tremendous amount of space to explore that. From starting to use AI in small ways in my early Creative coding class, to working on a system for automated website development for my creative coding final project (this was right before these tools started coming out onto the scene in force). Then exploring how to dive deeper into hardware development, including playing around with leveraging AI for circuit design and even having it help me with 3d printing. And then even exploring how to connect my AI directly to touch designer to find different ways of building with that more efficiently and effectively. And of course, playing around with these tools all at very early stages often meant spending more time trying to make the new tool work than it might have to just use tools the old way, but it felt like an active and ongoing research project into the class that Mark taught, "Future of Programming - LLMs?". This really turned a new leaf as I spent my last semester engaging in an independent study with Annie Margaret, in which I was actively working on building out a new AI learning curriculum that I called Learn, Vibe, Build. I've now begun teaching this on it's own separate from the university and will even be returning to CU in the fall to teach it as a class. And so I would say that one of my big outcomes of this program has been exactly that exploration. Is the future of programming LLMs? Yes it seems so, or at least they play a very large part in that future, as is being demonstrated by almost every major tech company and every new startup beginning to orient their entire developer ecosystem around more agentic coding workflows. And now I get to play a part in supporting more people, both at the university and beyond, learning how to orient well with these tools and to usher in a new generation of builders. So we could call that some degree of success in terms of intentions manifested…

But being curious about the future of learning is also a deep curiosity about how we think, and how we organize our minds. Of course this has showed up in my focus before entering CU, with Consciousness Hacking/Woven Web, and this exploration into the intersection of consciousness and technology. But it's also showed up in what has been another major focus of my life, and especially in recent years, a deep exploration of personal knowledge management and tools for thought. I think this is actually quite intimately connected to this exploration of learning, because my hypothesis is always that at the root of effective learning is being able to undertsand connections. That as cognitive beings we are always connecting dots and that this enables us to learn more effectively. And I've found that having better tools for thinking can help dots connect more easily. And when I'm talking about tools for thinking, I'm not always meaning some highly complex system. I think one of the most effective tools for thinking is simply just a little pocket notepad that we jot notes down, or a journal or diary where we return to each day to reflect. But the best thinkers, and the best learners, are known to have taken time to reflect on their own thinking and on their own learning. And so, finding myself back in school, where I'm in a structured learning environment, and where I want to tend to the lessons I'm learning in class, but also the larger lessons I'm learning in life, I found myself thinking even more again about tools for thought and personal knowledge management. And eventually decided that the thing that I have been wanting for years is actually mine to build. And so I began building it. And over the last two semesters, I've brought a tremendous amount of focus to building parachute, to building a knowledge system that helps me organize my thinking more effectively, and that is built to be an AI native tool so it benefits directly from all the new advances in artificial intelligence.

My final project has shown me a lot about how I work and where my edges are. For one thing, it's very clear to me that I am much more of a builder than I am a presenter, but that being able to present well and share with others is a vital skill for starting a company, and so that's an edge that I'm leaning onto. I've also seen that my tendency is generally to just go into solo build mode and create it, but through this process I've seen that it's important for me to find ways to include others, to grow the team but to also let myself share and receive feedback and advice. Working with Kurt was very helpful in this, as he was very supportive in general, but I found the more I was able to take time with him and share openly, the more he was able to help me refine the direction I'm going.

One thing I learned in this is about the importance of being able to pivot. With just about one month left in the semester, I had a moment of clarity in which I saw that I had been actually over-engineering a lot of this, and had become overly committed to one direction so much that I hadn't paused to consider whether it was the right direction. In that pausing, I saw there was a way to do this that was a lot simpler to start but that still built towards that larger vision I was going for. Once I realized this I actually stripped out almost all of the code I had been working on, and started fresh, bringing over a lot of the patterns we had worked on, but very little of the actual code. There was a big lesson for me in this about not adhering too much to the sunk cost fallacy, and being willing to change directions when it was needed. But there was another lesson, about how this willingness to change and pivot is more important than ever in the era of building with AI. With AI, it's actually quite possible to start over the entire thing from scratch (fresh code, sharing common patterns, even potentially using an entirely different language) with very little cost. And doing so can be quite effective if there's new clarity that's emerged because you are less beholden to decisions you had made in the past. And so I'm learning a lot about myself as a builder, building technologies and building organizations, but I'm also learning a lot about in general what it means to be a builder in this new era of building.

Reflecting a bit more on this arc. I've been curious about where cognition and technology can intersect; and my work in the world was reflecting this. I've also been very curious about civics and about our social systems and about how we can improve those; and further have been curious about where shifts in cognition and technology can support more connected social systems. And all of this has led me to a curiosity in how we learn and how we think and what can affect meaningful changes in how we think.

I've spent my time at ATLAS exploring the future of building, while simultaneously building better systems that support more effective creativity and learning. I've also doven deeper into my studies of extended cognition, placing it as an area of focus in the Neurohacking class I took with Annie Margaret. Studying extended cognition has directly contributed to my development of Parachute, a tool for thought that aims to foster a more connected and integrated worldview through creating a more integrated and interoperable tool for thinking.

The future of building found a nice peak in Marks Future of Programming - LLMs? class, and now I find myself teaching a version of this class, both outside of CU and in the fall I will teach it inside of CU.

All in all what this program gave me was a very necessary frame. A frame of a student of technology which permitted me to go much deeper in exploring what technology is and can be, and participating more actively in the development of it's next iteration. It has also given me a network, and I feel incredibly strengthened by being a part of the web that is CU, and especially of the web that is ATLAS. As I move into my next phase of life as a local engineer, entrepreneur and educator, I see myself continuing to be a strong part of ATLAS, benefiting from my home here but also contributing much more substantially to what it is and can be. I see ATLAS bringing forward the kind of education that is more essential than ever as we enter a new era of what it means to be a builder and I feel excited about helping to shape this next chapter with the many other amazing beings here.

## The first draft

*Written using Claude's outline as scaffolding, but with my own framing and voice. The opening I found — three prior chapters mapped to **t**echnology, **l**earning, and **s**ociety, all converging at **ATLAS** — was a structural move I came to on my own that I hadn't named in the free-write. # Completing the CU Chapter

I started my journey at CU almost two years ago now. In the years prior to this I had been working as a developer relations engineer at Google, and then following layoffs I stepped into an executive director role at Consciousness Hacking Colorado which came to be rebranded as Woven Web, and then I decided to run for city council in Boulder, accruing 3500 votes and feeding the flames of a long held interest in deepening my civic involvement. Interestingly enough, these 3 preceding chapters could be loosely defined along the dimensions of technology, learning, and society. And so it feels quite fitting that I would find myself stepping into my next chapter, as a masters student at ATLAS, the Alliance for Technology, Learning, and Society.

This was not my first masters degree. Before I started at Google, I was in a masters program at Naropa University, studying Ecopsychology. My final masters thesis for that was entitled Radical Reorganization, and it was exploring the concept of paradigms, and particularly the framing of our modern society as predominantly driven by a reductionist and mechanistic paradigm, while simultaneously exploring how we might shift towards a more relational and regenerative paradigm, in society at large but also in individual social systems; as well as the role each of us might play in that shift in our day to day lives. Just this last week, I posted a small reflection piece on that, on the 4 year anniversary of it being finished and published. It was entitled "Practicing Radical Reorganization" and it looked at the projects I am engaging in now, and how this theoretical thesis from 4 years ago has turned into a much more embodied practice in the present moment. My time at ATLAS has contributed greatly towards putting this all into practice. After all, technology is simply applied science, and by placing myself in a technology context for the last two years, I've enabled myself to much more directly apply many of the ideas that I have held and explored for some time.

This was one primary reason that I came to ATLAS; so that I might place myself in a technology frame for two years and be able to focus on my own creative direction in this arena. I have been a technology builder during many chapters of my life, but my masters degree at Naropa marked the tail end of a rather long period where technology was a much more background figure in my life. And I was ready for it to enter the foreground again. Particularly as technology has begun to advance with large language models and other AI technologies, it was an apt time for me to re-enter, and in doing so, to re-connect with a strong dharma that I hold, to play a role in helping to shape the future of technology, and with it, the future of learning and society.

I originally applied to the PhD program, having an interest in a combined PhD - Creative Technology & Design alongside Cognitive Science - to more deeply study connectedness and learning, and how we might develop more effective learning platforms. While I didn't get accepted into the PhD program, I did get accepted into the MS program and it turned out this was the perfect shape for me, enabling me to have a compact and dense two year program that let me focus on my learnings in technology, grow my skill set again in this changing era of building, and then graduate right at a time when I am more ready than ever to bring my creations to scale, to launch my startup, to launch an AI school, and to grow our beautiful cooperative we are tending to in downtown Boulder. And as I look ahead, it is also helpful to look behind. To look at the journey I have embarked on for the last 2 years and the themes woven throughout.

---

In my first semester, I took a class in Experimental practices, where I got to play around with various technologies to work with light and sound and video and sensors. I've always had a curiosity to explore more of this, as it feels like a way to truly bring technology into art. And while I can't say most of it came totally naturally for me, it's an area that I was excited to explore and that I'm happy to have a taste of. I also took design methods class, where I got to start learning how to use various designer tools such as Figma, and how to apply more design thinking to all my projects. It was helpful to have a direct context for this, and while I identify much more as a developer than a designer, it was nice to grow this skill set a bit and just to put myself in the context of it.

But the most exciting class for me in this first semester was Creative Coding. I first fell in love with coding because I could create with it and I loved one of my first ever homework assignments back in my freshman year of college, making a simple othello game, and how I got all the extra credit for the assignment because I went above and beyond to add in fun new features and customizations. This class brought me back to that simple love of coding, but it also gave me an opportunity to explore coding from a new perspective. It was during this class that I really started playing around with a new way of building, as LLMs had come onto the scene. And while I enjoyed getting back into some good old hand coding in this class, I more and more experimented with how to build more ambitious projects by leveraging AI to support. And by the end of the class I had built a final project around a tool for automatically generating websites with AI; something that became much more polished and common place in the months to follow, with tools like Lovable and Cursor and Windsurf entering the scene. It was actually after this class ended on a winter break that I first used a fully agentic editor with Windsurf and developed an entire application in an hour while also watching a Christmas movie with my family. This demonstrated to me quite clearly that there was a new kind of building and that exploring this was one of the most interesting directions for me to go in as the program progressed.

Fortunately, the program supported exactly that. The next semester I took a class entitled "Future of Programming - LLMs?" that was offered as an experimental class more than anything. An opportunity to explore together what programming could look like now that LLMs were getting increasingly better. This was literally just a sandbox choose your own adventure kind of course. And its actually one of the things I loved best about ATLAS; this was frequently an option. In almost any class, if you could demonstrate a clear pathway for you to go about learning the material in a way that worked better for you, most professors were open to letting you chart your own course. This was just a class that particularly demonstrated that.

This second semester also allowed me to explore the field of experimental practices more, in a class where we were regularly exposed to people who had made careers in experimental practices, which let me really see first hand the wide variety of possible life and career paths that one can pave for themselves; it was very inspiring. I also took creative technologies this semester, allowing me to start to play with hardware more. I got to also do some early experimenting in how AI can support hardware development, not just through writing arduino code, but through helping me design circuit boards and even directly generating 3d printer files for a project. I had a lot of fun in this class, especially with my final project developing a chess clock with an integrated camera to snap photos of a chess board as you played. I only got it to a very rough prototype but its certainly one of those projects I hope to touch back in on again as the technologies make the development of it even easier.

In my third semester I had one major project based class, with Design Studio. My groupmates and I developed a project called Biotelia. This was a really cool experiment at the intersection of art, ecology, and technology; an intersection I am particularly fond of. It involved tracking movement of people in the room but also of little robots, seeing both humans and robots as pollinators moving through an ecosystem. There were glowing lights and sounds that matched with the movements to create this embodied feeling of being a pollinator and being immersed in the natural world. This is something I love about being a technologist, like being an artist, the more you can refine your craft, the more you can take your visions and bring them directly into reality. In this case, it helped to be part of a really talented group of humans so we could all channel our collective talents and creativity into making this happen. This was not only a lesson in building collaboratively, but it allowed me to play around with building in multiple other angles. In this case, I got to dive deeper into the technology of Touch Designer, a node based programming language that supports creating interactive exhibits such as this. I had been challenged with this new technology when I first started using it the prior year, but this time around, I was able to see it as an experiment in new ways to use AI to develop, and I even found ways to connect my AI coding agent to TouchDesigner in a way that greatly accelerated my development and experimentation. Once again, it helps to be in an environment where we're encouraged to learn and explore new technologies; as I was able to continue to touch into this idea of what the future of programming is, which became an even more central theme as I moved towards completion of the program.

My other two classes in this 3rd semester were actually less technology focused. One class was community driven technology which I really appreciated as it emphasized more of the soft skills of being a technologist, of how to connect and communicate with a community to start from a place of understanding what needs built before diving right into the building. I really appreciated this framing;  as someone who loves building, I've often found myself leaping straight into creating, but more and more I am learning to pause and more deeply consider what needs built, both in my own consideration but also through relating with others. This lesson comes back in a big way in my final semester. Further, it gave me an opportunity to practice my speaking skills, something that shows up for me even more as I move towards what comes next after ATLAS, and starting my own company and raising funds.

The other class was Neurohacking, something that I really appreciated as it gave me a big space from the building as well, and let me settle more into the philosophy of all of it. This class focused on two angles, one was an exploration of consciousness and practice, an area which I've always deeply appreciated, and I even had the opportunity to teach Taiji to the class a few times. And the other was an exploration of intentional and calm technology, an area that I've been looking into a lot with Parachute but also with my general usage of technology, such as using the Daylight computer as my primary tablet, which I was excited to share with the class various times. This class also involved a section of independent study through selecting a book and reading it and presenting on it. This gave me an opportunity to dive into a book that I had been curious about for a long time called Extended Mind; which explored how the mind is not simply brain bound, but is rather Embodied, Situational, and Distributed. All of this has played a huge part in my ongoing thinking about technology, and particularly about the development of Parachute. A very helpful class for crystalizing the philosophy behind what I am building, something that I think is quite ssential.

So this third semester really prepared me in a big way, and in many ways it felt like this was my last semester of real class. Because in my fourth and final semester, I went almost entirely self-directed; as my two classes were an independent study and final project, both of which were focusing on projects that I was already working on, but that I was now able to bring extra focus to in my school context. For my independent study, I focused on an AI curriculum I had been developing called Learn, Vibe, Build. Here I got to directly play with a lot of the ideas that I have been exploring over the last 2 years and that we touched on in the "Future of Programming - LLMs?" class; looking at how to actually support people utilizing these tools well and becoming builders, even those who have had very little experience writing code, or even with very little technical computer skills whatsoever. During this semester, I ran an initial 4 week pilot cohort with 13 students and helped them get up to speed with the AI tools, and even helped many of them start shipping products for the first time in their lives. I refined this initial offering into a full 6 week cohort that now has 30+ students moving through it, and even built an entire platform to support them in the class. This is growing into a much larger vision of advancing accessible AI education. And it's even showing up in a return to ATLAS for me after graduation, as I will be returning to teach Learn, Vibe, Build at ATLAS in the Fall; supporting many CU students learning how to incorporate these tools better. This is a wonderful manifestation of my early intention to come to CU to study how we learn and to play a part in more effective learning, as now I will be at the university as an educator in a more formal context.

My other project in my final semester of course, has been Parachute. This has been my final project for class, and also my core focus for much of the last year, as I have been growing this project into a real product and a company, and as I move towards graduation, I am increasingly focused on growing Parachute and turning it into a sustainable business and a widely used product. What a gift it has been to be able to leverage my final project class not just to work on something meaningful to me, but to work on something that is real beyond just school. I think this has been the real gift of my time at ATLAS, that it hasn't been this separate education institution taking me away from the rest of my life. Rather it has created a frame that has enabled me to focus on the things that matter to me and grow my skill set so that I can be a more effective contributor on all of the projects that I care about. And now it continues to be a home where I can contribute in meaningful ways, as an educator, as an engineer, and even as an entrepreneur.

So.... What is Parachute? And why parachute?

I have been interested for a long time in the field of personal knowledge management. I started a daily journaling practice back in college around the same time I was also beginning to explore mindfulness practices and deepening into a real curiosity to understand myself and this world deeper. In my junior & senior years I even was building my own personal tool to organize my journals and other daily trackers, and I called it QuantifiedLife. While I lost the habit for a bit during my traveling years (2015-2020), over the last 3-4 years I've really picked it back up, and deepened in my interest of finding the right tool to help me organize my thinking more effectively. Over this time, I've played around with a few. I played a little bit with Notion, but the two that I've spent the most time with have been Obsidian and Tana. I've appreciated the flexibility each of these tools offers and the emphasis on networked knowledge, while at the same time I've found them both to be incredibly lacking in some far too vital ways. My constant back and forth between these two tools and the inability to fully commit to either of them helped make it clear to me that there truly was something missing here and that I was probably uniquely poised to build it. And so I began.

Parachute started with me thinking deeply about memory, and about idea of networked knowledge and knowledge graphs. I was thinking about how a graph can be a much more effective way of relating to and navigating knowledge, and how new AI tools could enable these structures to emerge in a far more customizable and therefore personal way, that also enabled for much more effectiveness. Ultimately the thing that I was interested in was addressing the massive fragmentation that I experience on the daily within our technology ecosystem, and that I feel rippling to affect my own psyche. One of the core ideas behind Parachute is that if we can foster a more interconnected digital thinking ecosystem, then it might just lead to a more connected natural thinking ecosystem, which I hypothesize would directly impact many of the major problems we face in the world today; not the least of which the monumental challenge we have of truly living well in an era where everything is trying to get us to speed up and produce more and more and more. This mission would actually become quite important and something that I would have to constantly return to, because in the goal of trying to build a thing to help us all slow down, it's quite easy to let my own sense of urgency take over, as it has again and again, and then I would find myself trying to create tools for balance while losing my own balance in the process. A lot of the thinking behind this has been deeply informed by my studies of extended cognition, because in many ways, what I am trying to build with Parachute is an extended mind tool, but what's also very clear is that for the extended mind to be effective, the original mind must also be effective.

The name Parachute comes from a Frank Zappa quote. He said that the mind is like a parachute, it doesn't work if it's not open. This thinking deeply informs my approach at Parachute, aiming to create an open tool for thinking that supports open minds.

Parachute has taken a few incarnations over the last year, but where it has landed is this. It is an integrated ecosystem for connected thinking. It's built on many modular components that make it very easy for the system to be self-assembling. The goal is for a persons Parachute to be uniquely their own, such that they can take a base model that just works but then transform it into something that is deeply personal. The starting point for this is the Parachute vault, which is the much more direct backend replacement for something like Tana and Obsidian; it is a way of storing your notes, tagging them to give them structure, and linking them together to support your thinking being more connected. This data is stored in a simple database, but it's surfaceable via a very flexible API which lets us build simple user interfaces around it but also allows any individual to create their own interface, with the plan for us making that really easy to do even for non-technical people. And it's also surfaceable via an MCP which means any AI system can be given access to this same knowledge vault. The idea here is that we can create a flexible enough knowledge and memory layer that any person can build together with their AI system to make it truly customizeable to their needs. This was just launched last week into an open beta, and I'm now working on new features such as the interface generator but also a personal agent interface, so that you can super easily spin up a knowledge bot that has access to a graph and place it into any chat you'd like, to add a memory and knowledge chat bot to wherever you do your conversing and thinking. We think this will be a really high value starting point that demonstrates a lot of the value of this whole parachute ecosystem. From here bridging to things like custom interfaces will add even more value; both for individuals trying to organize their thoughts but also for organizations, communities, and small groups.

I loved being able to continue to put into practice my own deepening growth in how to build using LLMs during this project, and I also learned a number of things that feel really relevant to this, both at the larger tool level but also about myself and how I build. One thing I got to explore a lot here is the balance of a solo-founder/solo-builder vs a good team player. In many ways, I find myself able to work a lot more effectively when I'm not trying to coordinate so much with a team, and so I inclined towards that in a lot of cases. However, I also have learned that doing so often means I'm missing several important perspectives, the kind of perspectives that are necessary to ensure I'm actually building the right thing, as I explore in community driven technology. It was in the times where I was really off in the wilderness building on my own that I got a lot done, but it was also in those times that I frequently wandered down the wrong direction for quite a long time. It was through returning to conversations with my other core collaborators, as well as with my advisor Kurt, that I was able to gain the perspective of where was the actual useful place to focus and where was I likely over engineering. I also found in this the value of knowing when to pivot. It's said often that good startups are willing to start over again and again and again. This can be quite an emotional journey, because we've invested so much into a particular direction. As I had when I finally came to terms about a month ago that I had been building in the wrong direction and I needed to largely start over from scratch with a simpler approach. However, once I actually committed and made the pivot, I found everything was moving more effectively. The new simpler shape was more communicable to my collaborators which let me bring others in more easily, but it also wasn't nearly as big of a lift as I thought. Which is really another one of the unique things about this new era of building with AI. We can often rebuild an entire application from the ground up in just a day or two, assuming we know with clarity what it is we need to build. And while I didn't retain almost any of the code from that first 8 months, what I did retain was a clear understanding of what needs built and what does not need built. And that meant that once I fully landed into this new direction. I was able to move with tremendous speed and clarity, and have found the last month that the product has moved along at a rate unlike it had before, and it's now had a soft launch and is even closer to a much more substantial launch coming soon.

---

Ultimately what I think I learned about myself is balance. I do have this pull towards slowness and relationality and embodiment, that came through in my Ecopsychology masters and that also shows itself a lot in my Taiji and other mindful practices. But I also have this pull towards acceleration and building and creativity, and that has shown up a lot more in these last two years of putting myself into a deeply building context. What I'm seeing more than ever is the importance of integrating these two parts. Learning how to go slow and smooth while still executing effectively in the world. This has been what I have been able to refine through not just these last 2 years, but through these last 6 years. 2 years at Naropa, 2 years in the school of life, and 2 years at CU. And now as one chapter closes and another one opens, I find myself valuing more than ever this balance that I have found.

For me what this looks like in practice is being willing to let myself go on these deep creative technology benders, staying up until 3am in the morning when the inspiration is really lit. But it also means listening to the signs my body is sending when I've been working too hard and losing the bigger perspective, and remembering to go for a long hike up the mountain or to prioritize time with friends. Technology has the potential to radically transform our world, but for it to do so in a beneficial way, we have to transform our relationship with it.

This is my vision for what ATLAS is and can be. A place where we transform our relationship with technology while also building the kind of technologies that can transform our relationship with life itself and make this world a better place. This is the vision I hope to continue to advocate for and work towards, as an alumni, as a community member, and soon as a lecturer. With gratitude< I close this chapter and open the next one.

~ Aaron G
