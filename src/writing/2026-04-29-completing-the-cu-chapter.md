I started my journey at CU almost two years ago. In the years prior, I had been working as a developer relations engineer at Google; following layoffs, I stepped into an executive director role at Consciousness Hacking Colorado (which we rebranded to Woven Web during my tenure); and then I ran for city council in Boulder, accruing 3,500 votes and feeding the flames of a long-held interest in deepening my civic involvement. These three preceding chapters could be loosely defined along the dimensions of technology, learning, and society. So it feels quite fitting that I would find myself stepping into my next chapter as a master's student at ATLAS — the Alliance for Technology, Learning, and Society.

This was not my first master's degree. Before I started at Google, I was in a master's program at Naropa University studying Ecopsychology. My thesis, [*Radical Reorganization*](https://unforced.org/writing/radical-reorganization/), explored the concept of paradigms — particularly the framing of our modern society as predominantly driven by a reductionist and mechanistic paradigm, and how we might shift toward a more relational and regenerative one, in society at large but also in individual social systems and in the role each of us plays day to day. Just last week, on the four-year anniversary of that thesis being published, I posted a small reflection piece called [*Practicing Radical Reorganization*](https://unforced.org/writing/practicing-radical-reorganization/), looking at how the theoretical work from four years ago has turned into a much more embodied practice in the present moment. My time at ATLAS has contributed greatly to that turn. Technology is, in many ways, simply applied science — and by placing myself in a technology context for the last two years, I've been able to much more directly apply many of the ideas I have held and explored for some time.

This was one primary reason I came to ATLAS: to place myself in a technology frame for two years and focus on my own creative direction in that arena. I have been a technology builder during many chapters of my life, but my Naropa master's marked the tail end of a long period where technology was a much more background figure for me. I was ready for it to enter the foreground again — particularly as large language models and other AI technologies began advancing in earnest. There was also a deeper reason. I came to ATLAS to grow the technology-builder half of myself so it could be in real dialogue with the relational and embodied half that Naropa had cultivated. I came to integrate. The dharma I hold around helping to shape the future of technology — and with it the future of learning and society — depends on those two halves actually informing each other.

I originally applied to the PhD program, with an interest in a combined PhD in Creative Technology & Design alongside Cognitive Science, hoping to study connectedness and learning more deeply and to develop more effective learning platforms. While I didn't get accepted into the PhD program, I did get accepted into the MS, and it turned out to be the perfect shape — a compact, dense two-year program that let me focus my learning, grow my skill set in this changing era of building, and graduate at exactly the right moment to bring my creations to scale: launching my startup, launching an AI school, and growing the cooperative we are tending to in downtown Boulder.

What stood out most about ATLAS for me was the freedom to chart my own course. In nearly every class, if you could demonstrate a clear pathway for engaging the material in a way that worked better for you, the professors were open to letting you do that. *Future of Programming - LLMs?* was the clearest expression of this — almost pure sandbox. The independent study and final project structure of the final semester let me bring my real-world work directly into the academic frame. ATLAS didn't ask me to put my actual life on pause to be a student; it gave me a frame in which the rest of my life could be focused. That's rare, and I valued it deeply.

---

My first semester gave me my first real exposure to what ATLAS is. I took *Experimental Practices*, where I worked with light, sound, video, and sensors and got a taste of bringing technology into art; *Design Methods*, where I picked up Figma and a more design-thinking-oriented approach to projects; and *Creative Coding*, which was the standout.

Creative Coding brought me back to my freshman-year love of coding — when I first fell for it because I could *create* with it, going above and beyond on a homework assignment to build a simple Othello game. This class returned me to that simple love. But it also gave me an opportunity to explore coding from a new perspective. LLMs had just come onto the scene, and I started experimenting with how to build more ambitious projects by leveraging AI as a partner. By the end of the class I had built a final project around a tool for automatically generating websites with AI — something that would become much more polished and common in the months to follow with tools like Lovable, Cursor, and Windsurf. On winter break after the class ended, I used Windsurf for the first time and built an entire application in an hour while watching a Christmas movie with my family. It demonstrated to me clearly that there was a new kind of building, and that exploring it would be one of the most interesting directions for me as the program progressed.

My second semester let me follow that thread directly with *Future of Programming - LLMs?*, an experimental class meant as a sandbox to explore what programming could look like now that LLMs were arriving in force. I also took *Futures in Experimental Practices*, where regular exposure to people who had built careers in experimental practices helped me see the wide variety of life and career paths one can pave. And in *Creative Technologies* I got to play with hardware more directly. I experimented early with how AI could support hardware development — not just writing Arduino code, but helping me design circuit boards and even generate 3D printer files. My final project there was [a chess clock with an integrated camera](https://unforced.org/writing/building-a-smarter-chess-clock/) that snaps photos of a board as you play.

In my third semester I had one major project-based class, *Design Studio*, where my groupmates and I developed a project called Biotelia. It was an experiment at the intersection of art, ecology, and technology — an intersection I'm particularly fond of. We tracked the movement of people in the room as well as little robots, treating both humans and robots as pollinators moving through an ecosystem. Glowing lights and sounds matched their movements to create an embodied feeling of being a pollinator immersed in the natural world. This is something I love about being a technologist: like an artist, the more you refine your craft, the more you can take your visions and bring them directly into reality. It helped to have a really talented group, so we could channel our collective creativity into making it happen — a lesson in building collaboratively. It also let me dive deeper into TouchDesigner, a node-based programming language for interactive exhibits. I had been challenged by it the prior year, but this time I found ways to connect my AI coding agent to TouchDesigner directly, which greatly accelerated my development.

My other two classes that semester were less about building technology and more about understanding it. *Community Driven Technology* emphasized the soft skills of being a technologist — how to connect and communicate with a community, and how to start from a place of understanding what needs to be built before diving in. As someone who loves building, I've often found myself leaping straight into creating; more and more I'm learning to pause and consider what actually needs building, both in my own thinking and in relationship with others. This lesson comes back in a big way in my final semester. The class also gave me an opportunity to practice my speaking skills — something that shows up for me even more as I move toward starting my own company and raising funds.

*Neurohacking* gave me space from the pure building work to settle more deeply into the philosophy of it all. The class explored two angles. The first was consciousness and practice — an area I've always cared about, and I even had the opportunity to teach Taiji to the class a few times. The second was [intentional and calm technology](https://calmtech.com/) — something I've been looking into a lot with Parachute and with my general usage of technology (the [Daylight Computer](https://daylightcomputer.com/) as my primary tablet, for example, which I was excited to share with the class). The class also included a self-directed reading section. I dove into *The Extended Mind*, a book exploring how the mind is not simply brain-bound but rather embodied, situated, and distributed. It has played a huge part in my ongoing thinking about technology, and particularly in the philosophy behind Parachute. A very helpful class for crystallizing what I'm building — something I think is essential.

This third semester really prepared me, and in many ways it felt like my last semester of real classes. In my fourth and final semester I went almost entirely self-directed; both of my classes were focused on projects I was already working on, but that I could now bring extra focus to in the school context.

For my independent study, I focused on an AI curriculum I'd been developing called Learn, Vibe, Build. Here I got to directly play with a lot of the ideas I'd been exploring over the last two years and that we'd touched on in *Future of Programming - LLMs?* — looking at how to actually support people in using these tools well and becoming builders, even those with little prior coding experience or technical background. During this semester I ran an initial four-week pilot cohort with 13 students and helped many of them ship products for the first time in their lives. I refined that into a six-week cohort that now has 30+ students moving through it, and I built an entire platform to support them. This is growing into a larger vision of accessible AI education. It's also showing up in a return to ATLAS for me after graduation — I'll be back this fall to teach Learn, Vibe, Build to CU students. A wonderful manifestation of my early intention to come to CU to study how we learn and to play a part in more effective learning, now in a more formal educator role.

My other project this final semester has been Parachute. This has been my final project for class, and also my core focus for much of the last year, as I've been growing it into a real product and company. As I move toward graduation, I'm increasingly focused on growing Parachute and turning it into a sustainable business and a widely used product. What a gift it has been to leverage my final-project class not just to work on something meaningful to me, but to work on something real beyond just school. I think this has been the real gift of my time at ATLAS: that it hasn't been a separate education institution taking me away from the rest of my life. It has been a frame that has enabled me to focus on the things that matter to me and grow my skill set so that I can be a more effective contributor on all of the projects I care about. And now it continues to be a home where I can contribute in meaningful ways — as an educator, as an engineer, and even as an entrepreneur.

## Parachute

So... what is Parachute? And why parachute?

I have been interested for a long time in the field of personal knowledge management. I started a daily journaling practice back in college around the same time I was beginning to explore mindfulness and a real curiosity to understand myself and the world more deeply. In my junior and senior years I built my own personal tool, QuantifiedLife, to track my journals as well as other daily stats. While I lost the habit a bit during my traveling years (2015–2020), I picked it back up over the last three to four years and deepened in my interest in finding the right tool to organize my thinking. I played with Notion briefly, but the two I've spent the most time with have been Obsidian and Tana. I've appreciated the flexibility each offers and the emphasis on networked knowledge — while at the same time finding both of them lacking in some far too vital ways. My constant back-and-forth between them, and the inability to fully commit to either, helped make it clear that there was something missing here, and that I was probably uniquely poised to build it.

Parachute started with me thinking deeply about memory, networked knowledge, and knowledge graphs — and about how AI tools could enable these structures to emerge in a far more customizable and personal way, and therefore far more effective. Ultimately what I was interested in addressing was the massive fragmentation I experience daily within our technology ecosystem, and that I feel rippling out to affect my own psyche. One of the core ideas behind Parachute is that if we can foster a more interconnected digital thinking ecosystem, it might just lead to a more connected natural thinking ecosystem — which I hypothesize would directly impact many of the major problems we face today, not least the monumental challenge of truly living well in an era where everything is trying to get us to speed up and produce more and more.

This mission has become quite important and something I've had to constantly return to, because in trying to build a thing that helps us all slow down, it is quite easy to let my own sense of urgency take over — as it has, again and again — and find myself trying to create tools for balance while losing my own balance in the process. A lot of my thinking here has been deeply informed by my studies of extended cognition. In many ways, what I'm trying to build with Parachute is an extended-mind tool, but it's also quite clear that for the extended mind to be effective, the original mind must also be effective.

The name Parachute comes from a Frank Zappa quote: *"The mind is like a parachute — it doesn't work if it's not open."* This thinking deeply informs my approach: trying to create an open tool for thinking that supports open minds.

Parachute has taken a few incarnations over the last year, but where it has landed is this: an integrated ecosystem for connected thinking, built on modular components that make it easy for the system to be self-assembling. The goal is for a person's Parachute to be uniquely their own — they can take a base model that just works, then transform it into something deeply personal. The starting point is the Parachute Vault, a backend replacement for something like Tana or Obsidian — a way of storing your notes, tagging them to give them structure, and linking them together to support more connected thinking. The data is stored in a simple database but is surfaceable via a flexible API, which lets us build simple user interfaces around it and lets any individual create their own. It's also surfaceable via an MCP, which means any AI system can be given access to the same vault. The idea is to create a flexible enough knowledge-and-memory layer that any person can build together with their AI to make it truly customizable to their needs. It just launched into open beta last week, and I'm now working on new features such as the interface generator and a personal-agent interface — so you can spin up a knowledge bot that has access to your graph and place it in any chat you'd like.

I learned a number of things about myself through this project. One was about the balance between solo-building and good team play. I find myself able to work more effectively when I'm not coordinating so much with others, and I inclined toward that often. But I also learned that doing so means missing several important perspectives — the kind necessary to ensure I'm actually building the right thing, as we explored in *Community Driven Technology*. It was in the times I was off in the wilderness building on my own that I got a lot done; it was also in those times that I frequently wandered down the wrong direction for a long time. Returning to conversations with my core collaborators, and with my advisor Kurt, was where I gained the perspective to see where to actually focus and where I was likely over-engineering.

This connects to one of the bigger lessons of the project: knowing when to pivot. It is said often that good startups are willing to start over again and again. This can be an emotional journey, because we've invested so much in a particular direction — as I had when I finally came to terms about a month ago that I had been trying to build too much at once, layering features over an unfinished foundation, when the simpler move was to ship the core (the Vault) first and let everything else grow outward from it. Once I committed to the pivot, everything began moving more effectively. The new shape was more communicable to my collaborators, which let me bring others in more easily, and it wasn't nearly the lift I'd feared. Which is one of the unique things about this new era of building with AI: we can often rebuild an entire application from the ground up in a day or two, assuming we know with clarity what it is we need to build. I retained almost none of the code from those first eight months. What I retained was a clear understanding of what needed to be built and what didn't. That meant once I landed in the new direction, I was able to move with tremendous speed and clarity. The product has moved in the last month at a rate it hadn't before, and is now in soft launch, with a more substantial launch coming soon.

## What I learned about myself

The project surfaced some specific patterns about how I work: that I lean solo by default but build better with collaborators in the loop, that I tend to over-engineer when I run unchecked, that I'm a builder more than a presenter and need to deliberately lean into the latter. But underneath those, the deeper thing I learned about myself is balance — and what it actually takes to live the integration I named at the start. I have a strong pull toward slowness, relationality, and embodiment, which came through in my Ecopsychology master's and which still shows up in my Taiji and other mindful practices. I also have a strong pull toward acceleration, building, and creativity, which has shown up much more in these last two years of putting myself in a deeply building context. What I'm seeing more clearly than ever is the importance of integrating these two parts: learning how to go slow and smooth while still executing effectively in the world. This is what I've been able to refine not just through these last two years, but through the last six — two at Naropa, two in the school of life, and two at CU. As one chapter closes and another opens, I find myself valuing this balance more than ever.

In practice, that looks like being willing to go on deep creative-technology benders, staying up until 3 a.m. when the inspiration is really lit. But it also means listening to the signs my body sends when I've been working too hard and losing the bigger perspective, and remembering to go for a long hike up the mountain or to prioritize time with friends. Technology has the potential to radically transform our world, but for it to do so in a beneficial way, we have to transform our relationship with it.

This is my vision for what ATLAS is and can be — a place where we transform our relationship with technology while also building the kinds of technologies that can transform our relationship with life itself, and make this world a better place. It's the vision I hope to continue advocating for and working toward — as an alum, as a community member, and soon as a lecturer.

The public face of this turn — what I'm building with Techne, Learn Vibe Build, and Parachute — is what I described last week in [*Practicing Radical Reorganization*](https://unforced.org/writing/practicing-radical-reorganization/). This paper is its inward complement.

With gratitude, I close this chapter and open the next one.
